Respiration Mechanics

Explore step-by-step biological processes for lungs and skin breathing below.

Inhalation

  • When the frog breathes through the nostrils, the mouth and glottis are closed and the floor of the buccopharyngeal cavity is lowered.
  • The low air pressure in the mouth cavity draws air into the buccopharyngeal cavity through the nostrils.
  • When the glottis opens, the nostrils close and the floor of the buccopharyngeal cavity is raised.
  • The increased air pressure pushes air into the lungs.

Exhalation

  • When the lungs contract, air is expelled from the lungs.
  • This is helped by the abdominal pressure and the elasticity of the lungs.
  • Some air is expelled through the nostrils while the rest is mixed with the air in the buccopharyngeal cavity.

1. Simple Diffusion

Oxygen ($O_2$) and Carbon Dioxide ($CO_2$) molecules move naturally from areas of high concentration to low concentration. This passive process is called simple diffusion. It allows the frog to exchange gases directly with the environment without spending any muscular energy.

2. Moist Mucus Surface

For diffusion to work, gases must first dissolve in liquid. The frog's skin contains specialized mucus glands that secrete a slimy, wet layer. Oxygen from the air dissolves into this moisture layer, enabling the molecules to pass smoothly through the cell membranes of the epidermis.

3. Rich Capillary Network

Directly beneath the frog's paper-thin outer skin lies a dense web of micro blood vessels called capillaries. As oxygen diffuses inward, red blood cells immediately capture the oxygen molecules and carry them away. This continuous blood flow keeps skin oxygen levels low, drawing even more oxygen in.

Frogs breathe through the mouth and lungs while in an active state.
